# Social Security Disability & Nebraska Medicaid Navigation Strategy

**For a 26-year-old male with Schizophrenia in Lincoln, NE**
**Date: February 25, 2026 | CRITICAL DEADLINE: March 1, 2026**

***

## Executive Summary

This report addresses two urgent, interconnected issues: (1) maintaining Nebraska Medicaid eligibility while covered under a parent's United HealthCare (UHC) plan, and (2) protecting a pending Social Security Disability claim while earning above the 2026 Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA) limit. Current monthly gross earnings of ~$1,950 exceed the 2026 SGA threshold of $1,690 for non-blind individuals. The March 1, 2026 CA DDS response deadline is the single most time-sensitive item. Failure to respond could result in the claim being closed and the loss of years of retroactive benefits dating to the 2020 filing.[^1][^2][^3]

**Critical context:** This claim was filed in 2020 and SSA/CA DDS has delayed a decision for approximately two years at the initial level. In the interim, Tom's trajectory — short employment (~2 months), homelessness (~2 months), inpatient/outpatient treatment, unemployment while living with his brother, and now a new job he is struggling to maintain — is not a liability. **This pattern is textbook evidence of inability to sustain substantial gainful activity**, and if framed correctly, it strengthens the disability claim significantly.[^4][^5]

### Why SSA's Delay Actually Helps the Claim

The two-year delay, while frustrating, has created a longer longitudinal record that demonstrates a **pattern of instability** — repeated failed work attempts, homelessness, cycling through treatment, and inability to maintain independence. SSA adjudicators and ALJs view this pattern as strong evidence of disability, particularly for Schizophrenia, because it shows the claimant actively tried to work and could not sustain it. The key is ensuring this narrative is properly documented and presented to CA DDS.[^6][^4]

## Part 1: Nebraska Medicaid & Coordination of Benefits

### Who Pays First: COB Rules

Under federal and Nebraska law, **Medicaid is always the payer of last resort**. When an individual has both Medicaid and private insurance (such as UHC), the private insurance is the **primary payer** and Medicaid is the **secondary payer**. This means:[^7]

- All claims must be submitted to UHC first.
- UHC processes and pays its share, issuing an Explanation of Benefits (EOB).
- Medicaid then pays remaining allowable costs (copays, deductibles, coinsurance) that UHC did not cover, up to the Medicaid allowable rate.[^8]
- The individual should never owe more than what Medicaid allows, because Medicaid "wraps around" the private insurance.

Nebraska's DHHS Coordination of Benefits (COB) program gathers, verifies, and maintains health insurance information for all Medicaid-eligible individuals. The individual **must report** the existence of the UHC plan to Nebraska Medicaid, including any changes to coverage. COB information can be reported by clients, DHHS staff, providers, or Managed Care Organizations.[^9]

### Risk of Losing Medicaid: Insurance Verification

Nebraska Medicaid requires members to respond to verification requests within **30 days**. If the state has requested the front/back of the UHC card and the individual fails to provide it by the deadline, Medicaid may terminate coverage for failure to provide required information. However, there is a **90-day grace period** — if coverage is lost for failure to respond, the individual can complete the verification within 90 days and have coverage reinstated without needing to reapply, provided they remain eligible.[^10][^11]

**Critical point:** Having private insurance does **not** disqualify someone from Medicaid. It actually benefits the state because UHC pays first and Medicaid pays less. The state wants to know about UHC so it can coordinate claims properly, not to terminate Medicaid.

### Nebraska Medicaid Insurance for Workers with Disabilities (MIWD)

Nebraska operates the **Medicaid Insurance for Workers with Disabilities (MIWD)** program, which is the state's equivalent of a "Medicaid Buy-In" or premium pay-in program. This program allows individuals with disabilities who are working to keep Medicaid even if their income exceeds standard Medicaid income limits.[^12][^13]

**Basic Coverage Group Criteria:**
- Income between 101% and 250% of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL)[^14]
- Resources at or below $4,000 for an individual[^14]
- Age 16 to under 65[^14]
- Has earned income, including self-employment[^14]
- Meets SSA disability criteria, or is determined disabled by the Nebraska State Review Team[^12]

**Key details:**
- Receipt of an SSDI payment meets the disability requirement automatically. For someone with a pending claim, the State Review Team can make an independent disability determination.[^12]
- There is **no separate application** — applicants submit a general Medicaid application and DHHS staff sorts them into the correct eligibility category, including MIWD.[^13]
- MIWD benefits are **identical** to traditional Medicaid benefits.[^13]
- A premium may apply if income exceeds 200% FPL, but premiums are capped at lower rates under the 2021 program changes.[^15]

At $1,950/month gross (~$23,400/year), this individual would fall well within 250% FPL for a single person (approximately $38,295 in 2026), making MIWD a viable fallback if standard Medicaid eligibility is ever questioned due to income.

### Nebraska Medicaid Work Requirements — May 2026

Nebraska is implementing Medicaid work requirements for the expansion population starting **May 1, 2026**. However, **individuals who are disabled are explicitly exempt**. The exemption list includes people with "disabling mental disorder" and those who are "medically frail or have special medical needs". A person with Schizophrenia who is applying for or receiving disability benefits should qualify for this exemption. Documentation of the disability (pending SSDI claim, medical records) should be submitted proactively to ensure the exemption is on file before May 1, 2026.[^16][^17][^18][^19]

## Part 2: Social Security & The SGA Conflict

### Current Earnings vs. SGA Threshold

The 2026 SGA limit for non-blind individuals is **$1,690/month**. At $15/hr and 30 hrs/wk, gross monthly earnings are approximately **$1,950**, which is $260 over SGA. If the SSA determines the individual is performing SGA at the time of adjudication, the claim will be denied at Step 1 of the five-step sequential evaluation — regardless of the severity of the disability.[^20][^21][^1]

**Reporting obligation:** Any changes in work activity — starting a job, changes in hours/duties/pay, or stopping work — must be reported to SSA while a claim is pending. The SSA will likely send a **Work Activity Report (Form SSA-821-BK)** when it learns of the employment. This form must be completed accurately and promptly.[^22][^23][^20]

### Tom's Full Work History — Framing It Correctly

Tom's employment timeline since the 2020 filing is actually a powerful asset when framed as a series of unsuccessful work attempts separated by disability-related breaks:

| Period | Activity | SGA Relevance |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| ~2020 | Disability application filed | Alleged onset |
| Job #1 | Employed ~2 months, then stopped | UWA candidate — under 6 months[^22] |
| ~2 months | Homeless | Disability-related gap — supports severity[^4][^24] |
| Treatment period | Inpatient/outpatient treatment | Medical evidence of severity |
| Unemployment | Living with brother, not working | Inability to sustain employment |
| Current (Feb 2026) | Just started new job, ~$1,950/mo | Struggling — potential second UWA |

This cycle of work → failure → homelessness → treatment → dependence → another work attempt is **exactly what SSA expects to see in severe Schizophrenia**. Employment gaps caused by disability are not weaknesses — they are evidence of instability and inability to sustain work. Failed work attempts that end because of the impairment actually improve credibility with SSA and ALJs.[^25][^5][^6][^4]

### Strategy 1: Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWE)

IRWE deductions are the most immediate tool to reduce countable earnings below SGA. SSA will subtract approved IRWE costs from gross monthly earned income when determining SGA.[^26][^1]

**IRWE eligibility criteria (all must be met):**
- The expense must be for an item or service needed to work due to the disability[^26]
- The individual must pay the cost out-of-pocket (not reimbursed by insurance or any other source)[^26]
- Payment must be in cash, check, or credit card[^26]
- The expense must correlate with the disabling condition[^26]

**Eligible expenses for Schizophrenia include:**
- **Prescription medications** (e.g., Lybalvi, Cobenfy) — specifically, the out-of-pocket copay or cost after insurance. The drugs or services must be "necessary to control the disabling condition to enable the person to work".[^27][^26]
- Therapy/counseling copays
- Transportation to medical appointments related to the disability
- Any other medically related items/services that allow the person to work[^27]

**IRWE math for this case:**

| Item | Monthly Cost (Est.) |
|------|-------------------|
| Gross monthly earnings | $1,950 |
| 2026 SGA threshold | $1,690 |
| Amount to reduce below SGA | $260+ |

The individual needs at least **$261/month** in approved IRWE to bring countable earnings below SGA. Medication costs for Schizophrenia are often substantial:

- **Cobenfy** list price: $1,887 for a 30-day supply without insurance. Even with insurance, copays can be significant.[^28]
- **Lybalvi** copay: May be $0 for first 3 fills with commercial insurance copay card, then as low as $20/fill with the savings program — but the savings card is **not valid for Medicaid recipients**. The out-of-pocket amount actually paid after all insurance is the deductible IRWE amount.[^29]

**Critical: Only the out-of-pocket amount paid by the individual counts as an IRWE**. If Medicaid or UHC covers the full cost, there is no IRWE deduction. Copays, deductibles, and coinsurance amounts paid by the individual are deductible.[^27][^26]

**IRWE applies to pending initial claims.** The SSA POMS (Program Operations Manual System) confirms that IRWE instructions apply to "persons in both the title II and title XVI programs who are either filing for benefits or already on the benefit rolls". IRWE can also be reported retroactively with supporting receipts.[^1][^26]

### Strategy 2: Unsuccessful Work Attempt (UWA)

Both Job #1 (~2 months) and the current job (if it ends within 6 months) can potentially be classified as **Unsuccessful Work Attempts**, which means SSA will not count those earnings against the disability claim.[^30][^22]

**UWA criteria (per SSA POMS DI 11010.145):**
- The work must last **no longer than 6 months**[^22][^30]
- Work must end, or earnings must drop below SGA, **because of the disabling impairment** (not layoff, personal choice, or unrelated reasons)[^31][^22]
- There must be a "significant break" — the individual must be out of work for at least 30 consecutive days, or forced to change jobs due to the impairment[^22]
- SGA-level work lasting more than 6 months **cannot** be classified as a UWA regardless of why it ended[^32][^22]

**Tom's prior job already qualifies as a UWA.** It lasted ~2 months (well under 6 months), and was followed by homelessness and treatment — clear evidence it ended due to disability. For work lasting 3 months or less, SSA generally accepts the documented allegation when the reason has a "reasonable relationship to the impairment". Work lasting 3–6 months requires stronger evidence (frequent absences, unsatisfactory performance, special accommodations, or symptom exacerbation).[^6][^22]

**The current job is building toward a second UWA.** If Tom cannot sustain this position and it ends within 6 months, the pattern of **two successive failed work attempts** separated by homelessness and treatment is extremely compelling evidence of disability.[^5][^4]

**Documentation requirements for UWA:**
- **Medical records** showing worsening symptoms or exacerbations around the time work ended[^31]
- **Doctor's notes** advising the individual to stop or reduce work[^31]
- **Employer statements** confirming the individual's limitations or reason for departure[^31]
- **Personal statements** detailing how symptoms prevented continued employment[^31]
- Completed **SSA-821-BK (Work Activity Report)** accurately describing both work attempts[^22]

**Key point for this case:** The DDS (Disability Determination Services) makes the final determination of whether work constitutes a UWA in initial claims. The CA DDS evaluator reviewing this claim will assess UWA. Every doctor visit, ER visit, missed workday, and symptom flare at the current job should be **documented in real time** — not reconstructed after the fact.[^22]

### Requesting Dire Need / Expedited Processing

Given that SSA has delayed this claim for ~2 years at the initial level, and Tom has experienced homelessness and medical instability during that time, the claim may qualify for **Dire Need (DRND) or Critical Case designation**. SSA flags cases for priority processing when the claimant faces an immediate threat to health or safety, including:[^33]

- **Homelessness** (past or current housing instability)[^24][^33]
- **Lack of access to medical care** due to financial hardship[^33]
- **Food insecurity**[^33]

SSA accepts the claimant's allegation of dire need "absent evidence to the contrary". Additionally, SSA specifically flags homeless applicants for expedited processing and includes special considerations for their ability to respond to correspondence. A **Congressional inquiry** — contacting Nebraska's U.S. Senator or Representative — is another effective way to push the claim forward, especially at this stage.[^34][^35][^24][^33]

### The March 1, 2026 CA DDS Deadline

This is the most urgent item. When the SSA or DDS sends a request for information (medical records, work activity report, questionnaire), they typically set a deadline — often 10–14 days for urgent requests or up to 30 days. Failure to respond by the deadline can result in:

- A decision being made based on available evidence (likely unfavorable)[^2]
- The claim being denied or closed for failure to cooperate[^2]
- Potential loss of the 2020 filing date and all associated retroactive benefits[^3]

If this deadline involves a request for records, a questionnaire response, or a consultative exam scheduling, the response must be submitted **before March 1**. If the individual cannot meet the deadline, he should **call the CA DDS examiner directly** and request an extension — DDS examiners have some discretion to grant short extensions if contacted proactively.[^2]

### SGA Strategy Comparison

| Strategy | Best If... | Key Risk | Timeline |
|----------|-----------|----------|----------|
| **IRWE deduction** | Out-of-pocket med costs ≥$261/month | Must document and report expenses to SSA; if costs are fully covered by insurance, deduction is $0 | Can be applied retroactively[^1] |
| **Unsuccessful Work Attempt** | Job ends within 6 months due to symptoms | Must prove impairment caused job to end — not poor performance or external factors[^22] | Must be documented in real-time |
| **Dire Need / Congressional Inquiry** | Claim has been pending 2+ years with homelessness/instability | Does not guarantee approval, but forces priority review[^33][^35] | File immediately |
| **Reduce hours** | Willing to cut to ~25 hrs/wk ($1,625/mo, below SGA) | Less income; employer may not accommodate | Immediate |
| **Combine IRWE + UWA** | Some out-of-pocket costs AND likely to stop working | Strongest protection if both are documented | Ongoing |

## Part 3: Practical Resources in Lincoln, NE

### Nebraska Vocational Rehabilitation (VR) — Lincoln Office

Nebraska VR helps individuals with disabilities prepare for, find, and keep employment. The Lincoln office can assign a VR counselor to develop an Individualized Plan for Employment (IPE) and connect the individual to supported employment services.[^36]

**Lincoln Service Office:**
- **Address:** 500 S. 84th St., Lincoln, NE 68510-2611[^37]
- **Phone:** 402-471-3231 | Toll Free: 800-472-3382[^37]
- **Fax:** 402-471-6309[^37]

Nebraska VR serves all disability groups except blind/visually impaired and provides individualized services including job coaching, training, and supported employment. Participating in VR services does **not** jeopardize a disability claim — in fact, SSA operates a Ticket to Work program that coordinates with VR.[^38][^36]

### Easterseals Nebraska — WIPA Program

The **Work Incentives Planning and Assistance (WIPA)** program, funded by SSA, provides free benefits counseling to disability beneficiaries. A Community Work Incentives Coordinator (CWIC) can produce a personalized **Benefits Summary and Analysis (BSA)** report explaining exactly how work and earnings affect SSDI, SSI, Medicaid, and other benefits.[^39][^40][^41]

**Contact:**
- **Provider:** Easterseals Nebraska[^39]
- **Address:** 2727 W. 2nd Street, Ste 471, Hastings, NE 68901[^39]
- **Phone:** 800-471-6425 or 402-462-3031[^39]
- **Hours:** 8:00–5:00 Monday–Friday[^39]
- **Website:** nebraskatickettowork.org[^41]
- **Service area:** Statewide, including Lancaster County (Lincoln)[^39]

**Note:** WIPA services are specifically designed for SSA beneficiaries. If the individual does not yet have an SSDI determination, he may not qualify for WIPA until approved. However, contacting Easterseals to discuss eligibility is still recommended.

### Disability Rights Nebraska (PABSS)

**Disability Rights Nebraska** is the federally mandated Protection & Advocacy (P&A) organization for the state. It provides legal representation, information, and referrals for individuals with disabilities, including Social Security-related issues.[^42]

- **Address:** 134 South 13th Street, Suite 600, Lincoln, NE 68508[^42]
- **Phone:** (402) 474-3183 | Toll Free: (800) 422-6691[^42]

They operate the **Protection and Advocacy for Beneficiaries of Social Security (PABSS)** program, which helps disability beneficiaries understand and navigate work incentives and employment-related issues.[^38]

### Legal Aid of Nebraska

**Legal Aid of Nebraska** provides free civil legal assistance to qualifying low-income individuals across all 93 Nebraska counties. They handle Social Security cases and can assist with Medicaid issues.[^43]

- **Website:** legalaidofnebraska.org[^43]
- **Services:** Information, referrals, advice, self-help services, and representation for civil legal issues[^43]

| Payer | Role | Action Required |
|-------|------|----------------|

**Important:** Providers should bill UHC first, then submit the EOB to Medicaid for secondary payment. If a provider only bills Medicaid, the state may reject the claim and require UHC to be billed first. Make sure all providers have both insurance cards on file.
