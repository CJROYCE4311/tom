## Nebraska Medicaid and Coordination of Benefits with Private Insurance
The administration of Nebraska Medicaid, known professionally as Heritage Health, functions under a rigorous "payer of last resort" statutory framework. This principle dictates that all other third-party resources, including private health insurance plans, must fulfill their legal and contractual obligations to pay for medical services before state Medicaid funds are utilized. In the case of a 26-year-old male covered as a "Disabled Dependent" under a parent’s United HealthCare (UHC/Haleon) plan, the private employer-sponsored insurance is unequivocally the primary payer.

### The Payer of Last Resort Doctrine and Order of Benefit Determination
The Coordination of Benefits (COB) is not merely an administrative preference but a legal requirement aimed at preventing the duplication of payments and ensuring the fiscal integrity of the Medicaid program. Under Nebraska’s Title 472 and Chapter 39 regulations, when an individual has both Medicaid and a private plan like the UHC/Haleon coverage, the private plan must adjudicate claims as if no other coverage exists. The secondary payer—in this case, Nebraska Medicaid—only considers the "Allowable Expenses" that remain after the primary insurer has issued an Explanation of Benefits (EOB).

The mechanism for this coordination involves a multi-step verification process. First, the provider must bill the primary insurance (UHC). Second, once the primary insurer processes the claim and determines the amount it will pay based on its negotiated rates and the member’s deductible and coinsurance requirements, it generates an EOB. Third, the provider submits this EOB along with a claim to the Nebraska Heritage Health plan (such as the UnitedHealthcare Community Plan of Nebraska, which is distinct from the commercial UHC plan). Medicaid then evaluates whether any balance remains that falls within its own "Would Pay Amount," effectively covering costs like copayments or deductibles, provided the total reimbursement does not exceed 100 percent of the Medicaid-allowable rate.

### Insurance Verification Risks and Administrative Deadlines
A critical administrative pressure point in the current context is the March 1, 2026, deadline for insurance verification. Nebraska’s Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS) requires the submission of physical or digital evidence of third-party coverage, typically the front and back of the insurance card, to its Third-Party Liability (TPL) unit. If this information is not provided by the deadline, the individual faces a significant risk of Medicaid termination.

This termination is often characterized as a "failure to cooperate" with the state’s efforts to identify liable third parties. Under the new policies established in early 2026, including the enforcement mechanisms of the "One Big Beautiful Bill" (HR 1), the state has a heightened mandate to ensure that Medicaid is not bearing costs that are the responsibility of private entities. A failure to respond to a request for information typically results in a 30-day notice of adverse action, after which the beneficiary may be disenrolled from Medicaid expansion or other Medicaid categories.

## Nebraska-Specific Programs for Working Beneficiaries
For individuals earning above standard Medicaid income limits, Nebraska offers specific pathways to maintain coverage, recognizing that individuals with chronic conditions like schizophrenia may need continuous access to high-cost care even when employed.

### Medicaid for Workers with Disabilities (MAWD)
The Medicaid for Workers with Disabilities (MAWD) program is a specialized eligibility group designed for individuals between the ages of 16 and 64 who meet the Social Security Administration’s medical definition of disability but are engaged in paid employment. MAWD allows for significantly higher income and asset limits than standard Medicaid expansion. In 2026, the income limit for MAWD is generally up to 250 percent of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL).

Beneficiaries in the MAWD program are often required to pay a monthly premium, which is calculated based on their family income and household size. This "Premium Pay-In" system ensures that the individual remains within the Medicaid ecosystem, which provides benefits—such as comprehensive behavioral health and lower prescription drug costs—that private commercial plans may not offer as generously.

### Disabled Persons and Family Support (DPFS) Program
For those who do not qualify for MAWD but still require financial support for disability-related expenses, the Disabled Persons and Family Support (DPFS) program provides up to $400 per month per eligible client, or $4,800 per 12-month period. This program utilizes a median family income guideline. For a single-person household in 2026, the median family monthly income guideline is approximately $5,433.00, which provides a substantial buffer for individuals earning $1,950 per month. DPFS is an auxiliary resource that can cover services or items not typically included in standard health plans, helping the individual maintain independent living and employment.

## Social Security and the Substantial Gainful Activity Conflict
The individual’s current gross monthly earnings of $1,950 present an immediate technical challenge to his pending Social Security disability claim, as this amount exceeds the 2026 Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA) threshold of $1,690. Because the application was filed in 2020 and remains in a pending status (likely at the hearing level or awaiting a decision from the California DDS), work at this level is viewed by the Social Security Administration (SSA) as prima facie evidence of an "ability to work," which can trigger a technical denial.

### The Reporting Process for Pending Claims
Income reporting is a mandatory responsibility for all disability claimants. For a pending claim, the report should be submitted to the Social Security field office currently holding the file, while also notifying the Disability Determination Services (DDS) examiner or the Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) if the case is at the hearing level. Given the geographic move from California to Nebraska, the individual must ensure that the SSA’s "Electronic Records Express" (ERE) system is updated so that work reports—specifically Form SSA-821 (Work Activity Report)—are associated with the correct case folder.

### The Unsuccessful Work Attempt (UWA) Framework
One of the most powerful work incentives available to claimants is the Unsuccessful Work Attempt (UWA). A UWA allows the SSA to disregard a period of work that was substantial enough to be considered SGA if the work effort ultimately failed or was reduced below the SGA level due to the claimant's impairment.

#### Criteria for a UWA in 2026
To qualify as a UWA, the work must meet three primary criteria:
1.  **A Significant Break:** There must have been a break in work for at least 30 consecutive days prior to the start of the work attempt, or the individual must have been forced to change to a different type of work or a different employer because of the impairment.
2.  **Duration of Six Months or Less:** The work effort must have lasted no more than six months. SGA-level work that lasts even one day beyond the six-month mark cannot be classified as a UWA under any circumstances.
3.  **Impairment-Related Cessation or Reduction:** The work must have stopped, or the earnings must have fallen below the SGA limit, because of the individual's impairment (in this case, Schizophrenia) or because "Special Conditions" that were essential to the work performance were removed.

#### Documentation for Mental Health Claims
For an individual with schizophrenia, documenting the reason for a work cessation requires a focus on functional limitations. The SSA accepts documented allegations when the reason for the work ending has a "reasonable relationship" to the impairment. If the individual is struggling with cognitive symptoms (e.g., disorganized thinking), hallucinations, or extreme social withdrawal that make a 30-hour work week unsustainable, these should be detailed on the SSA-821 form. If the individual’s employer provides a statement that the work ended due to these symptoms, or if the individual’s hours were reduced to below SGA (e.g., to 15 hours per week) due to a clinical decline, the entire period of work at the $1,950 level can be excluded from the SGA determination.

### Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWE)
Impairment-Related Work Expenses (IRWE) provide another vital mechanism to lower "countable" income. An IRWE is the cost of an item or service that the individual pays for out-of-pocket, which is necessary for them to work, and is directly related to their impairment. For an individual with schizophrenia who earns $1,950, identifying just $261 in monthly IRWEs would bring their "countable" earnings below the $1,690 SGA limit, thereby protecting the disability claim.

#### Schizophrenia-Specific IRWE Examples
The pharmacological landscape for schizophrenia in 2026 includes several high-cost, specialized medications. Drugs such as Lybalvi (olanzapine/samidorphan) and Cobenfy (xanomeline/trospium chloride) have list prices exceeding $1,600 per month. While most patients will have insurance (UHC or Medicaid) covering a portion of these costs, any remaining out-of-pocket copayments or deductibles qualify as IRWEs.

### Subsidies and Special Conditions
The SSA also recognizes "subsidies," which occur when an employer pays an individual more than the reasonable value of the actual services performed. If an individual with schizophrenia is given fewer duties, extra supervision, or allowed more frequent breaks than other employees in the same position, the SSA may determine that a portion of their $1,950 salary is actually a "subsidy". For instance, if an employer reports that the individual is only 80% as productive as a non-disabled peer, the SSA will only count $1,560 of the monthly earnings as SGA, which is safely below the $1,690 limit.
